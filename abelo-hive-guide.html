Before They Arrive
Pre-Arrival Tasks
Site, equipment, registration
Site Preparation
  • Choose a level, sheltered spot — south or south-east facing if possible, entrance facing away from the prevailing south-westerly wind
  • Erect a 2m screen, fence, or dense hedge directly in front of the hive entrance — forces bees to gain height immediately, taking their flight path over heads. Explicit SBA guidance for garden apiaries. At South Acre, boundary planting on north and east edges can serve this function
  • Hive stand essential — Abelo sell galvanised steel stands (you supply two 40×60mm wooden beams to complete them); any stable level platform works. Minimum 30cm off ground protects from damp and reduces back strain
  • Clear vegetation in front of entrance — bees need a clear flight path for at least 1 metre at ground level
  • Partial afternoon shade is beneficial in the rare event of Scottish summer heat — full sun all day stresses poly hives more than wooden ones as poly holds heat
  • Ensure you can approach from the side or rear — never from directly in the flight path
Equipment Checklist
  • Hive stand (Abelo galvanised or equivalent)
  • Floor — Abelo open mesh varroa floor with slide-out inspection tray and landing board (included with hive)
  • Brood box — holds 11 Hoffman self-spacing frames. Some beekeepers run 10 frames + 1 dummy board to make frame removal easier without rolling bees; both are valid
  • Crown board — check which your exact package includes before assuming it has a central fondant hole and feeder docking station. Abelo sell several configurations; verify against your actual parts before the bees arrive
  • Roof
  • At least 11 standard National frames — DN4 Hoffman self-spacing are a reliable choice for brood; any standard DN frame fits
  • Foundation or starter strips
  • Queen excluder — essential once you add a super; fits between brood box and super to keep the queen out of the honey. Abelo sell compatible plastic and wire versions
  • At least one super with frames (SN4) for when the colony establishes
  • Dummy board / follower board — useful for a nucleus colony starting small
  • Abelo Ashforth feeder — sits under the roof above the crown board docking station; bees access syrup via an internal slot aligned with the access point in the crown board dock
  • Protective suit or jacket with veil — no shortcuts here
  • Gloves — leather for beginners until confidence builds
  • Hive tool — J-type or standard
  • Smoker and fuel — corrugated cardboard, dried grass, wood shavings all work
  • Hive record book or app — Hive Tracks or Beekeeper's Companion
Understanding Your Abelo Ventilation System

Know this before the bees arrive. Your hive comes with plugs and ventilators for the roof and crown board holes.

🔵
White Polystyrene Plug
Fully seals ventilation holes. Cold and wet conditions. East Ayrshire winters.
🟢
Plastic Ventilator
Middle setting. Mesh-covered, weather excluded, some airflow maintained. Active season use.
🔓
Fully Open
Maximum ventilation. Rarely needed here. Only in exceptional summer heat.
📍
Local Practice, Not Gospel
The exact seasonal schedule (which plug, when) is site judgement — not an official Abelo prescription. Discuss with your mentor and local SBA members what works in Ayrshire.
Assembly Notes
  • Check all parts fit correctly before the bees arrive — brood box and floor may be pre-secured with 6 screws from underneath for transit; remove if not required
  • Entrance block in place, set to small entrance for a new colony — easier to defend. The Abelo entrance reducer is multi-functional: one orientation reduces width, inverted it closes further
  • Top and bottom of brood boxes and supers are reinforced with rigid plastic — make sure boxes are fully seated in the interlocking rebate system
  • When using your hive tool, always use a twisting motion in the integrated tool slots rather than levering directly against bare poly — levering can gouge and crack polystyrene over time
  • Paint is not required — Abelo hives come ready painted. If repainting, use exterior masonry paint in a light colour; never paint the interior
⚠️
Entrance Closure Warning
Only fully close the entrance for transport or very short confinement, and only when there is adequate ventilation elsewhere. Never seal an occupied hive airtight.
Registration & Support
  • Register with BeeBase (National Bee Unit) — free, essential for disease alerts: beebase.apha.gov.uk
  • Contact the Scottish Beekeepers' Association — Ayrshire division. Invaluable for local knowledge and swarm collection networks
  • Find a mentor if at all possible — your first season alongside an experienced beekeeper is worth more than any guide including this one

Colony Arrives
Day Zero
Transfer, settle, feed
🪄
Assumption
You're starting with a nucleus (nuc) — 5 frames of bees, brood, and a mated queen. This is the recommended route for beginners.
Arrival Sequence
  • Collect or receive your nuc in the evening or early morning when bees are calm and clustered
  • Transport with entrance closed (mesh or foam plug)
  • If the nuc is in a correx or cardboard travel box: either transfer frames promptly on arrival, or wait a full 24 hours. Don't stop at a few hours — bees orient to the wrong box
  • Transfer frames in order — lift each nuc frame directly into the brood box maintaining the same sequence; do not rotate or rearrange
  • Place nuc frames centrally in the brood box
  • Fill either side with your remaining frames (foundation or foundationless)
  • Add dummy board if space is large — reduces the void the bees need to heat
  • Crown board on. Roof on. Entrance reducer set to small opening.
  • Walk away. Resist the urge to look again until tomorrow.
Feeding
  • Feed 1:1 sugar syrup using the Abelo Ashforth feeder — but only once you see bees actively flying. If they haven't started drawing comb they cannot store syrup yet
  • The Ashforth feeder docks above the crown board under the roof — lift the roof to check and refill; no need to open the hive
  • Bees access syrup through the feeder's internal slot, which aligns with the access point in the crown board dock
  • The sliding hole on top of the metal grille — keep closed during feeding; open only to let bees clean the feeder once feeding is finished
  • In cold weather when syrup is unsuitable, fondant can be placed directly over the crown board central hole as an alternative
🌧️
East Ayrshire Spring
Assume they need feeding unless there's a strong visible flow. Cool, slow springs here mean colonies build later than southern UK equivalents — err on the side of supporting them.
🔭
Days 1–7
First Week
Observe, resist, first inspection
Days 1–5: Watch the Entrance
  • Avoid unnecessary disturbance — let the colony orientate and settle before any inspection
  • Bees flying with pollen is encouraging and usually means brood is being fed — but it is not proof that the colony is queenright. Only an inspection confirming eggs or young larvae tells you that
  • Check feeder levels every 2–3 days by lifting the roof only — do not open the hive
Day 5–7: First Inspection
  • Wait for above 14°C, dry, calm conditions — this is East Ayrshire; the window matters
  • Light the smoker before you approach — a few puffs at the entrance, wait 30 seconds, then open
  • Use your hive tool in the integrated tool slots on the Abelo boxes; always twist, never lever against bare poly
  • You are looking for: eggs, young larvae, capped brood, and ideally the queen herself
  • Eggs are tiny — a magnifying loupe helps enormously; they stand upright in the cell base like tiny white grains of rice
  • Don't panic if you can't find the queen — fresh eggs = she was there within the last 3 days
  • Check frames are being drawn; colony building in the right direction
  • Replace frames in order, crown board on, roof on
  • Record everything — date, weather, what you saw, any concerns
🌸
Weeks 1–4
First Month
Regular inspections, varroa baseline, build confidence
Regular Inspections
  • Inspect every 7–10 days in good weather (above 14°C, dry, calm) — don't inspect in cold, wet, or windy conditions
  • Continue feeding until the colony has drawn all frames and stores are visible — capped honey in upper corners of frames
  • Watch for queen cells — peanut-shaped wax structures usually on the bottom edge of frames; consult your mentor if you find them in a new nuc
Varroa Baseline Monitoring
  • Slide the inspection tray beneath the open mesh floor
  • Leave it in place for 7 days (not 3 — you need a steadier signal)
  • Count the natural mite fall to establish your baseline
  • The tray is unpainted polystyrene and stains quickly — a sheet of white Correx cut to size makes counting far easier and cleaner
Adding a Super
⏳
East Ayrshire Timing
Spring build-up is slower than southern UK equivalents. Only add a super when bees are covering 7–8 of the 11 frames solidly. A super added too early in a cold spring chills the brood. Fit the queen excluder beneath it when you do.
End of Month — What Good Looks Like
  • Colony building up steadily
  • Brood pattern solid — densely capped with few gaps
  • Bees covering most frames
  • Records up to date
🍯
The Long Game
Season & Beyond
Swarms, varroa, winter, spring — the full cycle
Regular Inspections (April–August)
  • Every 7–10 days during swarm season (May–July primarily)
  • Always checking: queen presence/evidence, brood pattern, stores, queen cells, signs of disease
  • Record every visit without exception
Swarm Management
  • Swarming is natural and will happen — your job is to monitor for it and have a plan before it does
  • Queen cells are your main warning sign
  • Methods to learn: artificial swarm (Pagden method) or removing queen cells with a split
  • Join the SBA — you will want experienced help the first time this happens
Varroa Management
  • Varroa destructor is present in virtually all UK colonies — this is not optional
  • Integrated Pest Management (IPM): monitor counts, treat when thresholds are reached
  • Licensed treatments: Apiguard (thymol, summer), MAQS (formic acid), Oxalic acid (winter, brood-free)
  • Use only VMD-authorised products and follow the label exactly — dosing matters. Overdosing oxalic acid can increase bee mortality and queen loss
  • Oxalic acid treatment in December/January when colony is brood-free is your most powerful annual tool
  • Keep records of all treatments including date, product, dose, and method
⚠️
Abelo Floor & Oxalic Sublimation
The Abelo varroa tray does not form a fully gas-tight seal when sublimating — vapour escapes around the edges, reducing effectiveness. For sublimation, supplement with foam blocks to seal gaps, or use the trickle/dribble method which doesn't require a sealed floor. This is a known design limitation.
🛡️
Sublimation Safety
Wear appropriate respiratory and eye protection, stand clear of the vapour plume. A sublimation wand's heat can damage polystyrene on contact — use a heat shield (scrap plywood or metal) on the mesh floor.
Feeding Calendar
🌱
March – May
1:1 sugar syrup (spring feed) — if stores are low or colony building on new frames. Use the Ashforth feeder.
🌻
August – September
2:1 sugar syrup (autumn feed, heavier — they store it) — ensure adequate winter stores. Colonies need approximately 15–20kg of stores to overwinter safely.
❄️
October – February
Fondant only in cold weather — place directly over the crown board central hole beneath the roof. Do not feed syrup when temperatures are low.
⚖️
Hefting Poly Hives
Poly hives are significantly lighter than wooden ones even when full. Calibrate your sense of a heavy hive by lifting it when you know stores are adequate — so you have a reference point. Light hive in winter = emergency fondant now.
Winter Preparation (October–November)
  • Switch roof ventilation to white polystyrene plugs for cold and wet conditions — exact timing is site judgement; discuss with local beekeepers what works in Ayrshire
  • Fit a dedicated metal mouse guard over the entrance — mice will nest in a warm poly hive. The Abelo entrance reducer can help but is not sufficient for long-term winter protection on its own
  • Strap the hive — South Acre's south-westerly exposure warrants it. The integrated handles on all four sides make strapping straightforward
  • Stop inspections when temperatures consistently below 10°C
  • Leave them alone until February/March unless hefting suggests starvation
Spring Assessment (February–March)
  • On a mild day (12°C+) — brief first inspection: signs of life, brood, stores
  • Begin feeding if needed
  • Clean and sterilise spare equipment during winter — Abelo poly parts wash well in washing soda solution; rinse thoroughly
Record Keeping — Always
  • Date and weather
  • Queen seen / eggs seen
  • Brood pattern
  • Stores
  • Varroa count
  • Any treatment (product, dose, method)
  • Behaviour notes and action required next visit
